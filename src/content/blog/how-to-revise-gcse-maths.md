Knowing *what* to revise is one thing — knowing *how* to revise effectively is what separates students who improve from those who spend hours at their desk and see no progress. This guide gives you a complete, practical GCSE Maths revision strategy built around what actually works.

## Why Most Maths Revision Does Not Work

The most common revision mistake students make is re-reading notes or watching YouTube videos without ever picking up a pen. Maths is not a subject you can learn passively. You have to practise it.

Reading a worked example and thinking "yes, I understand that" is not the same as being able to solve a similar question yourself under exam conditions. The only way to close that gap is through active recall — working through questions, making mistakes, and learning from them.

A second mistake is spending all your time on topics you already know because they feel comfortable. This is human nature, but it is counterproductive. Your exam grade is determined by the marks you pick up on weaker topics just as much as the marks on your strong ones.

## Step 1 — Know the Specification

Before you revise anything, know exactly what your exam board tests. The three main GCSE Maths boards are:

- **AQA (8300)** — the most widely sat, with three papers (one non-calculator, two calculator)
- **Edexcel (1MA1)** — Pearson's specification, three papers in the same format
- **OCR (J560)** — slightly different question style, same five topic areas

All three boards test the same five topic areas: Number, Algebra, Geometry and Measures, Statistics and Probability, and Ratio, Proportion and Rates of Change.

Download your exam board's specification (free from their website) and tick off every topic you cover during revision. This stops you missing something important.

## Step 2 — Prioritise by Topic Weight

Not all topics carry equal weight in the exam. For Higher tier, Algebra and Number together make up around 55–60% of the total marks. For Foundation tier, Number and Ratio are the heaviest areas.

Prioritise your revision time in proportion to how many marks each area is worth. A common mistake is spending weeks on circle theorems (Higher, low frequency) while neglecting percentage problems (both tiers, high frequency).

**High-priority topics for most students:**

- Percentages, fractions and decimals (Number)
- Solving equations, expanding brackets, factorising (Algebra)
- Pythagoras' theorem and trigonometry (SOH-CAH-TOA)
- Probability — tree diagrams, relative frequency
- Ratio and proportion

## Step 3 — Use Active Practice, Not Passive Reading

The most effective revision method for Maths is spaced practice with immediate feedback. Here is how to structure a revision session:

1. Pick one specific topic (e.g. solving simultaneous equations by substitution)
2. Work through 5–10 questions without looking at notes
3. Check your answers and study any mistakes carefully
4. Identify *why* you went wrong — was it a method error, arithmetic slip, or a gap in understanding?
5. Redo similar questions the next day to check the learning has stuck

This is called retrieval practice and it is consistently the most effective revision technique supported by research in cognitive psychology.

## Step 4 — Worked Example: Using the Revision Cycle

Say you are revising **simultaneous equations**.

**Question:** Solve the equations: 3x + 2y = 16 and x − y = 1

**Step 1:** From the second equation: x = y + 1

**Step 2:** Substitute into the first: 3(y + 1) + 2y = 16

**Step 3:** Expand: 3y + 3 + 2y = 16 → 5y = 13 → y = 2.6

**Step 4:** Substitute back: x = 2.6 + 1 = 3.6

**Answer: x = 3.6, y = 2.6**

After working through this, ask yourself: could you do it without notes? Could you do it in 3 minutes? If not, repeat similar questions the following day.

## Step 5 — Tackle Past Papers Under Exam Conditions

Once you have covered the main topics, past papers are your single most valuable revision tool. They tell you:

- Which topics appear most frequently
- How the exam board words questions (very different to textbook exercises)
- Whether your timing is realistic (1 mark per minute is a useful rule of thumb)

**How to use past papers properly:**

- Sit them timed, with the same conditions as the real exam — no phone, no notes
- Mark your own paper using the official mark scheme
- For every question you got wrong, find out *why* — method or arithmetic?
- Go back and revise that topic before doing the next paper

Working through 3–5 past papers in the final 4 weeks before your exam will improve your grade more than any other single activity.

## Step 6 — Build a Revision Timetable

Maths revision works best in short, focused sessions rather than long marathons. A session of 30–45 minutes on one specific topic is more effective than 3 hours of vague reading.

A simple weekly structure:

| Day | Session 1 | Session 2 |
|-----|-----------|-----------|
| Monday | Number practice | Check Monday's mistakes |
| Tuesday | Algebra practice | — |
| Wednesday | Geometry practice | Check Tuesday–Wednesday |
| Thursday | Statistics practice | — |
| Friday | Ratio & proportion | Mixed topic practice |
| Saturday | Past paper (timed) | Mark and review |
| Sunday | Rest or light review | — |

Adjust this to your own schedule, but keep sessions short and focused.

## Step 7 — Learn Key Formulae

Some formulae are given on the exam formula sheet (check your board's specification). Others you must know from memory. For Higher tier, common ones to memorise include:

- Area of a trapezium: A = ½(a + b)h
- Quadratic formula: x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac)) / 2a
- Sine rule: a/sin A = b/sin B
- Cosine rule: a² = b² + c² − 2bc cos A
- Volume of a cone: V = ⅓πr²h
- Volume of a sphere: V = ⁴⁄₃πr³

Make flashcards, test yourself regularly, and check you can apply each formula — not just recite it.

## The Week Before the Exam

Do not attempt to learn new topics in the final week. Instead:

- Sit one full past paper every day or every other day
- Review your marked papers each evening — focus only on topics you are still losing marks on
- Spend 10–15 minutes each day on mental arithmetic to keep your number skills sharp
- Get your equipment ready: calculator (check it is an approved model), compass, ruler, protractor

On the night before, review your formula flashcards and get an early night. Tiredness is one of the biggest performance killers in exams.

## A Note on Calculator Use

For the non-calculator paper, your mental arithmetic and written methods must be solid. Practise long multiplication, long division, and fraction calculations by hand regularly — not just on the calculator papers.

For the calculator papers, know your calculator well. Practice entering complex calculations — including fractions, powers, and trigonometric functions — efficiently without errors.
